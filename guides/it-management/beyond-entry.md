# 🚀 Beyond Entry-Level — IT Management Years 2+

> You landed your first management role — team lead, IT manager, or project manager. This is the map for what comes next, all the way to CIO.

[← Job Hunt](05-job-hunt.md) · [Hub](README.md)

---

## The honest progression

```
Team Lead / first-line manager  ───────────────────────────────  0–2 yrs
        ↓
IT Manager  (owns a function, a budget, a small team)  ─────────  2–5 yrs
        ↓
Senior IT Manager / Service Delivery Manager  ──────────────────  5–8 yrs
        ↓
IT Director / Head of IT  (org-wide IT strategy, multiple teams)  8–12 yrs
        ↓
CIO / VP of IT / CTO  (executive, board-level)  ────────────────  12+ yrs
```

The climb isn't just "more of the same with a bigger team." Each level shifts *what you're accountable for* — from running a process, to running a function, to setting strategy for the whole organization. The hardest transitions are the ones where the nature of the job changes, not just its scale.

---

## What each level adds

### Team Lead → IT Manager

- Move from coordinating a team's work to **owning a function end-to-end** — its services, its budget, its risk posture
- Your first real **budget ownership**: forecasting, variance, justifying spend to finance
- You stop being the best technician on the team and start being the person who makes the team effective
- The trap: staying "too technical" — doing the work instead of managing it. The shift from doer to multiplier is the hardest cultural change in this whole field.

### IT Manager → Senior Manager / Service Delivery Manager

- A **larger team or multiple teams**, often 8–20 people, sometimes with managers reporting to you (second-line management)
- **Vendor governance** at scale: quarterly business reviews, contract negotiation, SLA enforcement
- You own the **Change Advisory Board** and act as Major Incident Manager for the biggest events
- **Financial fluency becomes the gating skill** — managers who can't build a business case or hold their own in a budget conversation plateau here.

### Senior Manager → IT Director / Head of IT

- Responsibility for the **whole IT function**, not one slice — service, projects, infrastructure, and security all roll up to you
- You set the **annual IT roadmap** and defend it at the leadership table
- **People strategy:** org design, hiring plans, succession, developing your own managers
- You translate between the business and IT in both directions — turning strategy into IT capability and IT reality into business language.

### IT Director → CIO / VP / CTO

- **Executive, board-level accountability** for technology as a business enabler
- IT governance and **enterprise risk** — you own the answer to "is technology serving the organization's long-term interests and managing its risks?"
- Strategy horizon shifts to **multi-year**; you're making bets on platforms, sourcing models, and digital transformation
- Heavy on **stakeholder and political skill** — influencing peer executives, the board, and sometimes customers.

## The IC vs. management fork (and the architecture alternative)

Not everyone wants to manage people forever. There are three legitimate senior destinations:

1. **People-management track** — Team Lead → Manager → Director → CIO. Accountability for people, budgets, and strategy.
2. **Governance / strategy track** — specialize into IT governance (COBIT, CGEIT) or risk (CRISC, CISM). High-authority, narrower, advisory to the executive layer.
3. **Architecture track** — Enterprise Architect (TOGAF), shaping how IT capabilities are structured to serve business strategy. A senior individual-contributor path that influences without classic line management.

You don't have to choose at year two. The fork usually becomes real at the **Senior Manager → Director** stage, where deepening governance/architecture expertise and broadening people leadership start to pull in different directions.

## Senior-track credentials (Years 2+)

These are *destination* credentials — they require real experience and signal senior capability. Don't chase them early; pursue the one that matches your track once you qualify.

| Credential | Body | Track it serves | Experience needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PMP** | PMI | Project / delivery leadership | 36–60 months leading projects |
| **PRINCE2 Practitioner** | PeopleCert | Project (UK/EU/APAC) | Foundation first |
| **ITIL 4 Managing Professional / Strategic Leader** | PeopleCert | Service management leadership | Foundation + module path |
| **CISM** | ISACA | Security management | 5 years security mgmt |
| **CGEIT** | ISACA | IT governance / strategy | 5 years governance mgmt |
| **TOGAF Practitioner** | The Open Group | Enterprise architecture | No formal requirement |

> Full details, formats, and honest cost/confidence tags are in [certifications.md](certifications.md). Most of these are employer-paid once you're in a management role — sequence them to the track you're actually pursuing, not as trophies.

## What actually drives the climb

Credentials open doors, but progression past IT Manager runs on capabilities no exam tests:

- **Financial fluency** — building business cases, owning a P&L, speaking the CFO's language. The most common plateau is the technically strong manager who can't make the money case.
- **Executive communication** — presenting IT risk and strategy to a board in business terms, not jargon.
- **Building and keeping teams** — hiring well, developing your people, low regretted attrition. Directors are judged on the strength of the bench they build.
- **Strategic judgment** — making sourcing, platform, and investment bets that age well.
- **Political skill** — influencing peer executives and navigating organizational priorities without formal authority over them.

Invest in these continuously. They're the difference between an IT Manager who runs a tight ship and a Director who shapes where the ship goes — and they're best grown through [ongoing reading and communities](resources.md), not another certificate.
