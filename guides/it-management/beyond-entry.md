## the honest progression

```text
Team Lead / first-line manager: 0-2 yrs
  -> IT Manager: owns a function, budget, small team, 2-5 yrs
  -> Senior IT Manager / Service Delivery Manager: 5-8 yrs
  -> IT Director / Head of IT: org-wide IT strategy, 8-12 yrs
  -> CIO / VP of IT / CTO: executive, board-level, 12+ yrs
```

the climb is not just more of the same with a bigger team.
each level shifts what u are accountable for: process, function, strategy, then executive risk/value.

## what each level adds

### team lead -> IT manager

- [ ] own a function end to end: services, budget, risk posture
- [ ] first real budget ownership
- [ ] stop being best technician and start making the team effective
- [ ] avoid the trap of staying too technical and doing the work instead of managing it

### IT manager -> senior manager / service delivery manager

- [ ] larger team or multiple teams
- [ ] vendor governance at scale: QBRs, contracts, SLA enforcement
- [ ] Change Advisory Board ownership
- [ ] Major Incident Manager responsibility
- [ ] financial fluency becomes the gating skill

### senior manager -> IT director / head of IT

- [ ] whole IT function, not one slice
- [ ] annual IT roadmap and leadership-table defense
- [ ] people strategy: org design, hiring, succession, manager development
- [ ] translate business strategy into IT capability and IT reality into business language

### IT Director -> CIO / VP / CTO

- [ ] executive and board-level accountability
- [ ] IT governance and enterprise risk
- [ ] multi-year strategy horizon
- [ ] platform, sourcing, and transformation bets
- [ ] stakeholder and political skill with peer executives and board

## the IC vs management fork

not everyone wants to manage people forever.
there are three legitimate senior destinations:

1. **People-management track:** Team Lead -> Manager -> Director -> CIO.
2. **Governance / strategy track:** COBIT, CGEIT, risk, advisory power.
3. **Architecture track:** Enterprise Architect, TOGAF, business capability design.

u dont need to choose at year two.
the fork usually becomes real around Senior Manager -> Director.

## senior-track credentials

these are destination credentials.
dont chase them early.
pursue the one matching your track after u qualify.

| Credential | Body | Track | Experience needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PMP** | PMI | project / delivery leadership | 36-60 months leading projects |
| **PRINCE2 Practitioner** | PeopleCert | project, UK/EU/APAC | Foundation first |
| **ITIL 4 Managing Professional / Strategic Leader** | PeopleCert | service management leadership | Foundation + module path |
| **CISM** | ISACA | security management | 5 years security management |
| **CGEIT** | ISACA | governance / strategy | 5 years governance management |
| **TOGAF Practitioner** | The Open Group | enterprise architecture | no formal req, senior in scope |

full details are in [certifications.md](certifications.md).
most senior certs are employer-paid once youre in management.

## what actually drives the climb

credentials open doors, but progression past IT Manager runs on capabilities no exam tests.

- [ ] **Financial fluency:** business cases, P&L, CFO language.
- [ ] **Executive communication:** IT risk and strategy in business terms.
- [ ] **Building teams:** hiring well, developing people, low regretted attrition.
- [ ] **Strategic judgment:** sourcing, platform, investment bets that age well.
- [ ] **Political skill:** influencing peer executives without authority.

these separate an IT Manager who runs a tight function from a Director who shapes where the function goes.
grow them through real scope, [resources](resources.md), and communities, not just another cert.
