# 🎯 Phase 3: Pick a Track

> **~100 hrs total** · your pace, no deadline
> IT management isn't one job — it's five overlapping tracks. This phase is about going deep on the one that matches where you want to end up.

[← Phase 2](02-core.md) · [Hub](README.md) · [Next: Phase 4 — Portfolio →](04-projects.md)

## Why specialize now

Phases 1 and 2 gave you the shared foundation every IT manager needs. But "IT Management & Strategy" splits into five distinct sub-tracks, each with its own frameworks, certs, daily work, and senior destinations. You can't go deep on all five at once — and hiring managers want to see a clear lane.

This phase is about **choosing a primary track** and putting your specialization hours there — while keeping enough literacy in the others to collaborate. You'll drift between tracks over a career (most senior leaders have touched three or four), but for breaking in and for your portfolio, pick one and make it obvious.

### The five tracks at a glance

| Track | Core question | Key framework | Typical destination |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ITSM** | Are our services running well and improving? | ITIL 4 | Service Delivery Manager |
| **Project / Program** | Are we delivering this change successfully? | PMP / PRINCE2 | PMO Director |
| **Governance / Strategy** | Is IT serving the org and managing risk? | COBIT / TOGAF | CIO / Enterprise Architect |
| **People / Eng Management** | Is the team healthy and growing? | None (leadership craft) | Director of Engineering |
| **IT Operations** | Is everything up, fast, and secure? | ITIL + SRE | Head of Infrastructure |

Pick the row whose **core question** you most want to own. Read all five sections below once, then commit your hours to one.

## Track 1: IT Service Management (ITSM)

**You'll love this if:** you like process, predictability, and continuous improvement — turning chaos into a reliable, measurable service.

**What you go deep on:**
- The full ITIL 4 practice set beyond the big three: service request management, service level management, service configuration management, continual improvement
- The **ITIL 4 service value chain** and the four dimensions model, applied to a real service
- Designing a **service catalog** and a service portfolio
- Building a **CMDB** (configuration management database) concept and understanding why configuration data underpins good change management
- Continual improvement: running a structured improvement register, not just firefighting

**Certs that fit:** ITIL 4 Foundation (or ITIL Foundation v5 — see [certifications.md](certifications.md)) → ITIL 4 Specialist: Create, Deliver & Support → ITIL 4 Managing Professional.

**Daily reality at senior level:** Service Delivery Manager / IT Service Manager. You own service-desk health, the major-incident process, vendor SLAs, and the continual-improvement backlog. You live in dashboards and QBRs.

**Specialization labs:**

| Lab | Platform | What you do | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Build a full service catalog | Notion or Confluence (free) | Design 10 services with owners, SLAs, request paths, and costs | **Free** |
| Configure ITSM workflows | ServiceNow Developer Instance (free PDI) | Set up request, incident, and change workflows with SLA timers | **Free** |
| Run a continual-improvement register | Notion | Log 10 improvement opportunities, prioritize, and track 3 to "done" | **Free** |

**Practice gate:** you can walk through the ITIL 4 service value chain on a real service and explain how service configuration data enables safe change management.

## Track 2: IT Project & Program Management

**You'll love this if:** you like defined outcomes, deadlines, and the satisfaction of shipping a complex change on time — migrations, rollouts, transformations.

**What you go deep on:**
- A full delivery methodology: **PMBOK** (PMI) or **PRINCE2** (stronger outside North America) end to end
- **Program management** — coordinating multiple related projects toward a strategic outcome
- **Portfolio prioritization** — MoSCoW, weighted scoring, selecting which projects get funded
- **Earned value management (EVM)** — measuring project health by cost and schedule variance
- **Agile at scale** — SAFe or Scrum-of-Scrums when delivery spans many teams
- **Benefits realization** — proving the project actually delivered the value it promised

**Certs that fit:** CAPM (entry) → PMP (the gatekeeper cert in enterprise/government) or PRINCE2 Foundation → Practitioner. PSM I if you're running agile delivery. See [certifications.md](certifications.md).

**Daily reality at senior level:** Senior PM → Program Manager → PMO Director. You govern delivery across a portfolio, run steering committees, and own the relationship between IT delivery and business sponsors.

**Specialization labs:**

| Lab | Platform | What you do | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Run a multi-stage project plan | Asana or Jira (free) | Plan a fictional infrastructure migration with milestones, dependencies, and a critical path | **Free** |
| Build a portfolio prioritization model | Google Sheets | Score 8 candidate projects with weighted criteria; recommend which 4 to fund | **Free** |
| Audit the Google PM Certificate | Coursera (audit free) | Complete the planning, execution, and Agile modules; keep the artifacts | **Free** (audit) |

**Practice gate:** you can choose between waterfall and agile for a given project with a defensible rationale, and explain how earned value reveals a project is in trouble before the deadline slips.

## Track 3: IT Governance & Strategy

**You'll love this if:** you're drawn to the big-picture questions — aligning IT spend with business goals, managing enterprise risk, and designing how technology capabilities serve strategy.

**What you go deep on:**
- **COBIT 2019** (ISACA) — the governance framework: governance vs. management objectives, the goals cascade, maturity assessment, risk appetite
- **TOGAF** (The Open Group) — enterprise architecture: the Architecture Development Method (ADM), capability architecture, how IT structure serves business strategy
- **IT risk management** at the enterprise level (ISACA's CRISC domain)
- **IT strategy & roadmapping** — multi-year capability planning, technology investment portfolios
- **Compliance & audit readiness** — evidence management, control frameworks, working with auditors
- **SFIA** as a capability-planning tool for the whole IT function, not just yourself

**Certs that fit:** COBIT 2019 Foundation (entry to governance) → CGEIT (the landmark governance cert) or CRISC (risk). TOGAF Foundation → Practitioner for the architecture lane. CISM if your governance includes security. See [certifications.md](certifications.md).

**Daily reality at senior level:** IT Governance Lead → Enterprise Architect → IT Director → CIO. You report IT risk and value to the board, own the multi-year roadmap, and make the build-vs-buy and invest-vs-retire calls.

**Specialization labs:**

| Lab | Platform | What you do | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Read the COBIT 2019 intro | ISACA (isaca.org/resources/cobit) | Study the governance/management split and the goals cascade | **Free** intro |
| Walk the TOGAF ADM | The Open Group (opengroup.org/togaf) | Read the ADM overview; sketch a capability architecture for a fictional company | **Free** overview |
| Build a 12-month IT roadmap | Miro or Linear (free) | Create a themed IT roadmap (infrastructure, security, tooling) with rationale and dependencies | **Free** |

**Practice gate:** you can explain the COBIT distinction between governance and management, and sketch how an enterprise-architecture view connects a business goal to the IT capabilities that serve it.

## Track 4: People & Engineering Management

**You'll love this if:** you get more satisfaction from growing people and building a healthy team than from any individual technical win.

**What you go deep on:**
- This track is **less framework-driven and more craft-driven**. Your curriculum is the canonical management literature, not a certification body.
- **The IC → manager transition** — the hardest cultural shift in this field (stop doing, start enabling)
- **1:1s** — running effective one-on-ones, the core management ritual
- **Performance management** — feedback, reviews, growth plans, and handling underperformance (including PIPs)
- **Hiring** — writing role definitions, structured interviewing, reducing bias
- **Team structure & topology** — how to organize people around the work
- **Delegation & prioritization** — knowing what to do, delegate, or drop
- **Psychological safety & accountability** — the culture that makes teams perform

**The reading list is the curriculum:** *The Manager's Path* (Camille Fournier), *An Elegant Puzzle* (Will Larson), *Staff Engineer* (Larson, for the IC-vs-management fork), plus Lethain.com ongoing. See [resources.md](resources.md).

**Certs that fit:** honestly, almost none carry real weight here. This track is proven by track record and references, not credentials. PSM I or an ITIL Foundation can show process literacy, but management ability is demonstrated, not certified.

**Daily reality at senior level:** Engineering/IT Manager → Senior Manager → Director of Engineering. You spend your time in 1:1s, hiring loops, cross-team coordination, and removing obstacles for your team. You write very little code or config — and that's the point.

**Specialization labs (artifacts, since there are no sandboxes for management):**

| Lab | Platform | What you do | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Write a 1:1 framework | Notion or Google Docs | Design your 1:1 structure, recurring questions, and how you'd track growth themes | **Free** |
| Draft a role definition + rubric | Google Docs | Write a job description and a structured interview rubric for an IT role you'd hire | **Free** |
| Build a team health-check | Atlassian Team Playbook (free) | Run the Health Monitor play on a fictional team; identify the top 2 interventions | **Free** |

**Practice gate:** you can describe the IC→manager mindset shift in concrete terms and explain how you'd structure a 1:1 and a hiring loop to be effective and fair.

## Track 5: IT Operations

**You'll love this if:** you like keeping the lights on, infrastructure, and the satisfaction of a system that's fast, reliable, and secure.

**What you go deep on:**
- **Infrastructure & platform management** — networks, servers, cloud environments, endpoints
- **Monitoring & observability** — what to measure, alerting that doesn't cry wolf, dashboards
- **SRE practices** — SLIs/SLOs/error budgets, toil reduction, automation-first operations
- **Incident command** at scale — running major incidents, on-call rotations, escalation design
- **Capacity & availability management** — planning for growth, designing for the availability the business actually needs
- **Disaster recovery & business continuity** — RTO/RPO, DR testing, backup strategy
- **Security operations basics** — patching cadence, vulnerability management, the ops side of security

> This track sits closest to the [IT Support & Networking](../it-support-networking/) and [Cloud & DevOps](../cloud-devops/) guides — the difference is that you're *managing* the operation (people, process, budget, SLAs), not doing the hands-on engineering. If you want the hands-on technical depth itself, those guides go deeper; this track is the leadership layer on top.

**Certs that fit:** ITIL 4 Foundation for the service/ops process layer; vendor ops certs (covered in the Cloud/IT-Support guides) for technical depth; PagerDuty/SRE practices are learned, not certified. See [certifications.md](certifications.md).

**Daily reality at senior level:** Infrastructure Manager → Head of Platform / Head of Infrastructure. You own uptime, the on-call program, capacity planning, the DR strategy, and the infrastructure budget.

**Specialization labs:**

| Lab | Platform | What you do | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Design an on-call program | PagerDuty (free, ≤5 users) | Set up an escalation policy, a rotation, and alerting rules; document the major-incident roles | **Free** |
| Study the SRE workbook | Google SRE (sre.google/books) | Read the SLO and toil-reduction chapters; define SLOs for a fictional service | **Free** |
| Build a DR plan outline | Notion or Google Docs | Define RTO/RPO for 3 systems and outline a tested backup/restore strategy | **Free** |

**Practice gate:** you can design an on-call escalation policy, define SLOs with an error budget for a service, and explain RTO vs RPO in a disaster-recovery plan.

## How to choose (and how committed you are)

You're not signing a lifetime contract. But for the next ~100 hours and your Phase 4 portfolio, pick one track and commit:

- **Most accessible entry on-ramp:** ITSM and IT Operations (you can grow into these from a service-desk or sysadmin role).
- **Clearest credential ladder:** Project/Program (CAPM→PMP) and Governance (COBIT→CGEIT).
- **Hardest to fake, highest ceiling:** People/Engineering Management — but you usually need to have led people first.
- **Most strategic / executive-facing:** Governance & Strategy.

> A useful test: of the five **core questions** in the table at the top, which one would you be happiest answering every single day for the next five years? That's your track.

Your portfolio in Phase 4 should lean toward your chosen track — but include at least one cross-track artifact (everyone benefits from a post-mortem and a business case) so you read as a well-rounded manager, not a narrow specialist.
