## The honest entry reality (read this first)

**This is the one IT field that genuinely hires from zero.** A+ certified + a documented home lab + decent communication is enough to land a first help-desk role at most employers. You don't need a degree or prior tech job.

The realistic ladder:

```
Help Desk T1 / Service Desk Analyst
   (6–18 mo: tickets, password resets, triage, customer comms)
        ↓
Desktop Support / NOC Technician / Junior Sysadmin
   (1–3 yr: hands-on hardware, AD, monitoring, escalation ownership)
        ↓
Network Admin / Sysadmin / Cloud or Security pivot  (2–4 yr)
        ↓
Network/Systems Engineer · Cloud Engineer · Security Analyst  (4+ yr)
```

Target the **first row** for your first job. Help desk isn't a dead end — it's the launch pad for networking, cloud, security, and sysadmin careers.

> **AI is compressing pure T1 headcount at large enterprises** (chatbots deflect password resets). Position above pure ticket-handling from day one: own escalations, script repetitive fixes, pursue cloud/identity skills. **MSPs and smaller orgs are less automated and remain strong hiring grounds.**

## Resume & profiles

- **Lead with certs + home lab**, then skills, then any customer-facing experience (retail/service counts — it proves communication)
- **Quantify:** "Built a Windows domain lab with a DC + 3 clients", "Automated bulk user creation with PowerShell, cutting a 30-min task to 2"
- **Mirror posting keywords** for ATS — "Active Directory, Office 365, ticketing, troubleshooting, TCP/IP" must appear (truthfully)
- Link your GitHub; pin the lab write-ups
- **Headline example:** `Aspiring IT Support Specialist | CompTIA A+ | AD & PowerShell home lab`
- **Soft skills are a real filter here** — help desk is a customer-service job with tech attached. Show patience and clear communication.

## Job titles to search for

True-entry (target these):
- Help Desk Technician / Service Desk Analyst / IT Support Specialist
- Desktop Support Technician
- NOC Technician
- Technical Support Representative

Achievable next step (after the above, or with a strong lab + CCNA):
- Junior Systems Administrator
- Junior Network Technician
- Field Service Technician

> A first role titled "Help Desk" is the standard entry door. Don't skip it waiting for a "Network Engineer" title — that comes after.

## How to find and target roles (region-agnostic)

Job boards are mostly local, so use a strategy that works anywhere:

1. **MSPs are the #1 entry point.** Managed Service Providers hire T1 in volume and expose you to dozens of client environments fast — you learn more in a year than most do in three elsewhere.
2. **Company-direct.** List 20–40 target employers — MSPs, mid-size companies with internal IT, healthcare, schools/universities, government contractors. These have steady T1 hiring and lower automation pressure than big tech. Check careers pages directly.
3. **Referrals beat applications.** Engage in communities (see [resources.md](resources.md)) — r/sysadmin, r/ITCareerQuestions, local IT meetups.
4. **Filter for remote** for help-desk/NOC roles (many MSPs are fully distributed). Note: desktop support, field service, and network-tech roles are on-site by nature.
5. **Tailor every application** — attach or link the home-lab project most relevant to that role.

## Interview prep

See the full **[Interview Prep Q&A bank →](interview-prep.md)** — technical questions (hardware, OS, TCP/IP, AD, ticketing) + the all-important behavioral/customer-service prompts.

### Quick hits — questions you *will* be asked
- A user can't connect to the internet — walk me through troubleshooting (start at layer 1!)
- What happens when you type a URL and press enter? (DNS → TCP → HTTP)
- DHCP vs static IP — when each? What does DHCP actually do?
- A user forgot their password / is locked out in AD — what do you do?
- Difference between a hub, switch, and router?
- Subnet this: how many hosts in a /26?

### Behavioral (the real filter for support)
- "An angry user is yelling about a problem you can't immediately fix — what do you do?"
- "You don't know the answer to a user's question — walk me through it."
- "A time you explained something technical to a non-technical person."

> **Help-desk interviews weight customer-service behavior as heavily as technical skill.** Practice staying calm, empathetic, and methodical.
