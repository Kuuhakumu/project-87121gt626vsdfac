# 🎤 Interview Prep — UI/UX Design

> Format guide + question bank. Design interviews almost always include a portfolio walkthrough — nail that first, then drill the rest.

[← Job Hunt](05-job-hunt.md) · [Hub](README.md)

---

## Interview formats by role

| Role | Typical pipeline |
|---|---|
| **Product Designer** | Portfolio review → app critique or whiteboard exercise → design process deep-dive → cross-functional fit |
| **UI / Visual Designer** | Portfolio review → visual design critique → tool exercise (Figma live or take-home) → brand/style alignment |
| **UX Researcher** | Portfolio review (studies, not screens) → research methods discussion → analysis exercise → stakeholder influence |
| **Content Designer / UX Writer** | Portfolio/writing samples review → content critique → writing exercise (draft microcopy for a scenario) → content strategy discussion |

> Every role starts with a portfolio walkthrough. Your ability to narrate your own process — clearly, confidently, without jargon — is what carries most interviews. Practice until it feels natural.

## Portfolio walkthrough (the most important format)

You'll be asked: **"Walk me through a project."**

Structure that works in 5 minutes:
1. **Context** — what was the product, who are the users, what was the business problem? (30 sec)
2. **Your role** — what were you specifically responsible for? (15 sec)
3. **The research** — what did you do to understand the problem, what did you find? (60 sec)
4. **Key decisions** — pick 2–3 moments where you made a real choice between alternatives, and explain why (90 sec)
5. **The outcome** — what shipped, what did you measure or learn, what would you do differently? (60 sec)

> Don't just narrate what you did — show your thinking. Interviewers want to see your judgment, not just your deliverables. "I tried X, it failed usability testing, so I pivoted to Y because of Z" is a better answer than "I designed the final screen."

**Follow-up questions you should expect:**

- "Why did you make that decision rather than [the obvious alternative]?"
- "What would you do differently if you had more time / a bigger team?"
- "How did you handle pushback from engineering / PM / stakeholders?"
- "What did users tell you in testing? Were there surprises?"
- "What would the next iteration look like?"

**Practice gate:** you can walk through your strongest case study in 5 minutes, answer 3 follow-up questions, and say "I don't know, but here's how I'd find out" for anything you genuinely don't know — before walking into an interview.

## App critique / product teardown (whiteboard format)

One of the most common formats at product companies: *"Walk me through how you'd improve [product]."* Or: *"Critique [app] — what would you change?"*

Structure that earns points:

1. **Clarify** — what user type are we optimizing for? What problem are we trying to solve? What's success?
2. **Observe** — walk through the current experience and name what you see (good and bad). Don't just criticize; identify what works.
3. **Prioritize** — pick *one* problem area. Don't try to redesign the whole product.
4. **Propose** — sketch (literally or verbally) one solution. Be concrete.
5. **Trade-offs** — what does your solution give up? What would you need to validate?

> The most common mistake is jumping to solutions immediately. Interviewers want to see that you understand the problem before reaching for a fix.

**App critique questions to practice:**

- "How would you improve the checkout flow for [e-commerce app]?"
- "The add-to-cart button isn't performing well — walk me through how you'd investigate and address that."
- "Critique the onboarding of [app you use daily]. What works? What doesn't?"
- "You're redesigning the notification center for a mobile banking app. Where do you start?"
- "A competitor just shipped a feature your PM wants to copy. How do you evaluate whether to copy it?"

## Design process questions

These probe your understanding of *when* and *why* to use each method — not just whether you know the terminology.

**Research methods:**

- "When would you use a usability test vs a survey?"
  (Usability test: evaluate how well a design works; observe behavior. Survey: measure attitudes and prevalence at scale. Different questions need different methods.)
- "You have 2 days and no budget for user research. What do you do?"
  (5-second test on a colleague, hallway usability test with 3–5 people, expert review against heuristics, existing analytics/support tickets.)
- "How do you communicate research findings to a team that doesn't read reports?"
  (Short video clips of key moments, a one-page visual summary, live readout in a design review.)
- "What's the difference between generative and evaluative research?"

**IA and interaction design:**

- "Walk me through how you'd structure the information architecture for a new feature."
- "What's a design pattern you've borrowed from another domain and why did it work?"
- "Describe a microinteraction that solved a real user problem."
- "How do you decide when a modal is appropriate vs an inline pattern?"

**Accessibility:**

- "What's WCAG 2.2 AA and why does it matter?"
  (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines — 4 principles: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust. AA is the minimum legal/regulatory bar in most jurisdictions. It matters because 15–20% of people have a disability affecting digital product use — and because accessible design is usually better design.)
- "How do you bake accessibility into your process rather than bolt it on at the end?"
  (Color tokens for contrast, semantic component naming, focus order in Figma, Stark plugin checks during design, written success criteria before dev handoff.)
- "Name one WCAG 2.2 criterion you commonly see violated."
  (Common answers: color contrast below 4.5:1, non-descriptive link text like "click here," missing focus indicators, images without alt text.)

**Design systems:**

- "What makes a good component in a design system?"
  (Clear name, documented variants and states, accessibility built in, single source of truth in Figma with a matching code component.)
- "How do you handle a one-off design that doesn't fit the existing system?"
- "What's a design token and why does it matter?"

## The AI tools question (2026)

You will be asked: **"How do you use AI tools in your design work?"**

Good answer signals:
- *I use generative tools for visual exploration — moodboards, reference images, icon variations — to move faster early in a project.*
- *I use LLMs to draft copy for edge cases: error messages, empty states, tooltips. I edit for voice and accuracy.*
- *I treat AI output as a starting point. Design decisions still need user evidence behind them — I don't ship something because a model generated it.*

Don't claim deep prompt engineering or AI specialization unless you've actually done it. Don't pretend AI doesn't exist — every design team uses these tools. The signal interviewers want: you use it pragmatically, not uncritically.

## Take-home design exercise tips

Most entry-level product design roles include a take-home exercise: a 24–72 hour brief. Graders look for:

- **A clear problem statement** at the start — what are you solving for?
- **Evidence of a research step** — even a quick competitive analysis or 3 questions you'd ask users
- **2–3 key decisions** documented with alternatives you considered and why you chose your direction
- **Outcome framed honestly** — what did you validate, what would you test next?
- **Clean, legible Figma** — labeled frames, organized components, no junk layers
- **A short written summary** (one page or one Notion doc) — your rationale in plain language

> Time-box it. A focused 6-hour submission with clear thinking beats a sprawling 20-hour one with no conclusions. If the brief says "spend no more than X hours," believe it — going over does not impress.

## Behavioral (STAR) prompts

Every round will include behavioral questions. Use the format: **S**ituation → **T**ask → **A**ction → **R**esult. Lead with the result if it's strong.

- "Tell me about a time your research changed the direction of a project."
- "A stakeholder pushed back on a design decision you were confident in. What did you do?"
- "Walk me through a project that didn't go as planned — what did you learn?"
- "How do you handle receiving design critique that you disagree with?"
- "Tell me about a time you had to simplify a design significantly — what drove the decision?"
- "Describe a collaboration with an engineer that improved the final product."
- "Tell me about a time you had to advocate for the user against business pressure."

> Don't panic if you don't have a perfect story for every prompt. "I haven't run into this exact situation yet, but here's how I'd approach it" is a perfectly honest answer that shows self-awareness — better than making something up.

## The question you should ask them

Always end with real questions — not "What's the team culture like?" (generic) but:

- "What does good design feedback look like on this team?"
- "How does design interface with the PM and engineering process here — where does design influence the roadmap?"
- "What's the biggest design challenge the team is working on right now?"
- "What would make someone in this role successful in the first 90 days?"

These signal that you've thought seriously about the role, not just about landing it.
