# interview prep: UI/UX design meow

> design interviews almost always include a portfolio walkthrough.
> nail that first, then drill app critique, process questions, take-homes, and behavioral prompts.

## interview formats by role

| Role | Typical pipeline |
|---|---|
| **Product Designer** | portfolio review -> app critique or whiteboard -> design process deep dive -> cross-functional fit |
| **UI / Visual Designer** | portfolio review -> visual design critique -> Figma exercise -> brand/style alignment |
| **UX Researcher** | portfolio review with studies, not screens -> methods discussion -> analysis exercise -> stakeholder influence |
| **Content Designer / UX Writer** | portfolio/writing samples -> content critique -> microcopy exercise -> content strategy discussion |

every role starts with a portfolio walkthrough.
your ability to narrate your own process clearly, without hiding in jargon, carries most interviews.

## portfolio walkthrough

youll be asked: **"Walk me through a project."**

structure that works in 5 minutes:

1. **Context** - product, users, business problem. 30 sec.
2. **Your role** - what u specifically owned. 15 sec.
3. **The research** - what u did and found. 60 sec.
4. **Key decisions** - 2-3 real choices between alternatives. 90 sec.
5. **The outcome** - what shipped, what u measured or learned, what youd do differently. 60 sec.

dont just narrate tasks.
show your thinking.
"i tried X, it failed testing, so i pivoted to Y because of Z" is stronger than "i designed the final screen."

### follow-up questions to expect

- [ ] "Why did you make that decision rather than [the obvious alternative]?"
- [ ] "What would you do differently with more time or a bigger team?"
- [ ] "How did you handle pushback from engineering, PM, or stakeholders?"
- [ ] "What did users tell you in testing? Were there surprises?"
- [ ] "What would the next iteration look like?"

**practice gate:** u can walk through your strongest case study in 5 minutes, answer 3 follow-up questions, and say "i dont know, but heres how id find out" when thats the honest answer.

## app critique / product teardown

common prompt: **"Walk me through how youd improve [product]."**
or: **"Critique [app]. what would u change?"**

structure that earns points:

1. **Clarify** - which user type, what problem, what success metric?
2. **Observe** - walk through the current experience and name what works and what doesnt.
3. **Prioritize** - pick one problem area. dont redesign the whole product.
4. **Propose** - sketch or describe one concrete solution.
5. **Trade-offs** - say what the solution gives up and what u would validate.

most candidates jump to solutions too fast.
interviewers want to see that u understand the problem before reaching for the fix.

### app critique prompts

- [ ] "How would you improve the checkout flow for an e-commerce app?"
- [ ] "The add-to-cart button isnt performing well. walk me through how youd investigate and address that."
- [ ] "Critique the onboarding of an app you use daily. what works? what doesnt?"
- [ ] "Youre redesigning the notification center for a mobile banking app. where do you start?"
- [ ] "A competitor shipped a feature your PM wants to copy. how do you evaluate whether to copy it?"

## design process questions

these test when and why to use each method, not just whether u know the term.

### research methods

- [ ] **When would u use a usability test vs a survey?**
  usability test evaluates how well a design works by observing behavior. survey measures attitudes or prevalence at scale.
- [ ] **You have 2 days and no budget for research. what do u do?**
  hallway usability test with 3-5 people, 5-second test on a colleague, expert review, existing analytics/support tickets.
- [ ] **How do u communicate findings to a team that doesnt read reports?**
  short clips, one-page visual summary, live readout in design review.
- [ ] **Whats the difference between generative and evaluative research?**

### IA and interaction design

- [ ] "Walk me through how youd structure IA for a new feature."
- [ ] "Whats a design pattern youve borrowed from another domain and why did it work?"
- [ ] "Describe a microinteraction that solved a real user problem."
- [ ] "How do u decide when a modal is appropriate vs an inline pattern?"

### accessibility

- [ ] **Whats WCAG 2.2 AA and why does it matter?**
  Web Content Accessibility Guidelines. four principles: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, Robust. AA is the minimum legal/regulatory bar in many places.
- [ ] **How do u bake accessibility into the process?**
  contrast-aware color tokens, semantic component naming, focus order, Stark checks during design, success criteria before dev handoff.
- [ ] **Name one WCAG 2.2 criterion u commonly see violated.**
  color contrast below 4.5:1, vague link text, missing focus indicators, images without alt text.

### design systems

- [ ] **What makes a good component in a design system?**
  clear name, variants and states, accessibility built in, single source of truth in Figma with matching code component.
- [ ] "How do u handle a one-off design that doesnt fit the system?"
- [ ] "Whats a design token and why does it matter?"

## the AI tools question

u will be asked: **"How do you use AI tools in your design work?"**

good answer signals:

- [ ] u use generative tools for visual exploration: moodboards, reference images, icon variations
- [ ] u use LLMs to draft edge-case copy, then edit for voice and accuracy
- [ ] u treat AI output as a starting point, not a final answer

dont claim deep prompt engineering unless youve actually done it.
dont pretend AI doesnt exist.
the signal is pragmatic use with judgment.

## take-home design exercise tips

entry-level product design roles often include a 24-72 hour brief.
graders look for:

- [ ] clear problem statement at the start
- [ ] evidence of a research step, even quick competitive analysis or questions youd ask users
- [ ] 2-3 key decisions with alternatives considered
- [ ] honest outcome: what u validated and what ud test next
- [ ] clean Figma: labeled frames, organized components, no junk layers
- [ ] short written summary with rationale in plain language

time-box it.
a focused 6-hour submission with clear thinking beats a 20-hour sprawl with no conclusions.
if the brief says "spend no more than X hours," believe it.

## behavioral prompts

use STAR: **Situation -> Task -> Action -> Result**.
lead with the result if its strong.

- [ ] "Tell me about a time your research changed the direction of a project."
- [ ] "A stakeholder pushed back on a design decision you were confident in. what did u do?"
- [ ] "Walk me through a project that didnt go as planned. what did u learn?"
- [ ] "How do u handle design critique you disagree with?"
- [ ] "Tell me about a time u had to simplify a design significantly. what drove the decision?"
- [ ] "Describe a collaboration with an engineer that improved the final product."
- [ ] "Tell me about a time u had to advocate for the user against business pressure."

dont panic if u dont have a perfect story.
"i havent run into that exact situation yet, but heres how id approach it" is honest and better than inventing.

## questions to ask them

ask real questions, not generic culture filler:

- [ ] "What does good design feedback look like on this team?"
- [ ] "How does design work with PM and engineering here? where does design influence the roadmap?"
- [ ] "Whats the biggest design challenge the team is working on rn?"
- [ ] "What would make someone in this role successful in the first 90 days?"

these show u are thinking about the work, not just trying to land any offer.
