# 📚 Resources — UI/UX Design (2026)

> Curated, current, mostly free. Quality over quantity — you don't need 40 tabs open.

---

## Start-here picks (if you only use a few)

- **Visual fundamentals:** *Refactoring UI* (Wathan & Schoger) — the best practical UI-design book for people learning by doing
- **UX theory:** *Don't Make Me Think* (Krug) — read this before anything else; about 3 hours, and it'll change how you see interfaces
- **Practice:** [Daily UI](https://www.dailyui.co) + [Sharpen.design](https://sharpen.design) — build habits through repetition
- **Portfolio feedback:** [ADPList](https://adplist.org) — free 1:1 sessions with working designers and researchers
- **Community:** [r/UXDesign](https://www.reddit.com/r/UXDesign/) — honest feedback, job-search reality checks, no filler

## Free courses & learning tracks

| Resource | Covers | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| [Google UX Design Professional Certificate](https://www.coursera.org/professional-certificates/google-ux-design) | Full UX process: research → wireframes → hi-fi → portfolio | Free w/ financial aid (~$294 otherwise) |
| [Interaction Design Foundation](https://www.interaction-design.org) | 40+ courses: IA, research methods, interaction design, design thinking | ~$156/yr ⚠️ (confirm at ixdf.org) |
| [freeCodeCamp](https://www.freecodecamp.org) | Basic UX/UI content and web fundamentals — supplementary | Free |
| [Figma Learn](https://help.figma.com/hc/en-us/categories/360002051613) | Official Figma tutorial library: components, auto layout, variables | Free |

> CareerFoundry and General Assembly offer structured bootcamp-style tracks, but the market is saturated with bootcamp graduates. The portfolio matters more than the program. Verify current pricing on their sites before committing — both use dynamic pricing.

## Books worth owning

| Book | For |
|---|---|
| *Don't Make Me Think* — Steve Krug | Everyone — the foundational usability book; start here |
| *The Design of Everyday Things* — Don Norman | Everyone — where the mental model for UX as a discipline comes from |
| *Refactoring UI* — Adam Wathan & Steve Schoger | UI/visual design — the best practical guide to making things look right |
| *100 Things Every Designer Needs to Know About People* — Susan Weinschenk | Research-backed psychology applied to design decisions |

> You don't need all of these at once. Start with Krug and Wathan/Schoger in Phase 1 — Norman and Weinschenk will deepen your research and critique skills in Phase 2 onward.

## Stay current

- **Figma Blog** (figma.com/blog) — product updates, Config conference content, design system thinking
- **Nielsen Norman Group Articles** (nngroup.com/articles) — research-backed UX articles; free; high quality
- **IxDF Articles** (interaction-design.org/literature) — topic-based reading lists, free to browse
- **r/UXDesign weekly threads** — ground-level market signal; filter for "portfolio feedback" and "job hunting"

> On AI tools: in 2026 you're expected to know that generative design tools exist (Figma AI, Framer AI, Midjourney for moodboards) and to have an opinion on when to use them. The honest take: they accelerate visual exploration; they don't replace the judgment needed to know *what* to design or *why*. Researchers and strategists are the least threatened; visual production work is the most assisted.
