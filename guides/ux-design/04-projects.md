## What a UX portfolio must prove

When hiring managers look at a junior portfolio, they're asking three things:

1. **Do you have a process?** (You didn't just make pretty screens — you figured out what to make and why.)
2. **Can you make decisions?** (You chose this layout/flow/copy for a reason you can explain.)
3. **Can you communicate?** (A non-designer can follow your case study and understand what you did.)

> The #1 portfolio mistake: a Behance page full of polished mockups with no context. Beautiful screens with no research, no problem framing, and no explanation of tradeoffs show *execution*, not *design thinking*. Execution might get you a UI freelance gig. Decision-making and process get you a full-time Product Designer role.

---

## What a case study must contain

Each portfolio piece should tell this story:

1. **The problem** — what user need or business goal was this solving? Where did the problem come from (brief, research finding, metric)?
2. **Your role** — what were you responsible for? (Be specific and honest. "I did everything" is less credible than "I owned the research and information architecture; the visual design was collaborative.")
3. **The research** — what did you find out? How? At least one artifact: an affinity map, a user interview quote, a usability test finding. Connect it to a decision.
4. **The design decisions** — show at least two versions of a key screen and explain *why* version B won. The reasoning is the work.
5. **The outcome** — what happened? Usability test results, a metric that moved, stakeholder sign-off, or even "we learned X and pivoted." Honesty is fine; silence is not.
6. **What you'd do next** — what would iteration 2 address? This shows product thinking, not just execution.

## Case study types (aim for 3–4 across types)

| Type | What it demonstrates | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **End-to-end product redesign** | Full process from research to hi-fi | Best centerpiece piece. Pick a real app or website with real problems. |
| **0→1 new feature** | Discovery, ideation, scoping | Choose a genuine unmet need — not a feature that already exists. |
| **Usability audit + fix** | Evaluative research + iteration | Fast to produce, credible if your analysis is sharp. Pair with a Stark accessibility audit. |
| **Accessibility-focused redesign** | WCAG 2.2 knowledge + systematic thinking | Rare in junior portfolios — strong differentiator. |
| **Design system or component** | Systems thinking, consistency at scale | More relevant for Product Designer and UI tracks. Show the rationale, not just the output. |

## What gets REJECTED vs what gets HIRED

> **Rejected:**
> - Fake problems ("I redesigned Airbnb for fun") with no real user data and no evidence anyone has that problem
> - "Process theater" — a diagram of the double diamond with no actual research artifacts
> - UI-only-for-UX — a beautiful mockup with no case study, no problem, no evidence of a process
> - Template sameness — Figma Community UI kit + pastel color scheme + lorem ipsum. Reviewers have seen a thousand of these.
> - Case studies where nothing was tested, nothing changed, and the first design was also the final design
> - Portfolios where every project looks identical (same layout, same color, same component style) — suggests you're applying a template, not solving different problems

> **What gets hired:**
> - A case study where you found a real problem through real research and can defend every decision
> - Evidence of iteration — something changed because you tested it
> - An accessibility audit or compliance check done systematically
> - A research artifact that shows actual data (quotes, affinity map, usability test video clip)
> - Honest caveats: "Given more time, I'd have tested the navigation with a tree study"
> - Projects that span a realistic scope — a 3-screen feature with great depth beats a 40-screen app with no depth

## The Daily UI project (parallel, not sequential)

Run [Daily UI](https://www.dailyui.co) alongside your main case studies. It's a 100-day email challenge sending one prompt per day. Use it to:
- Build craft velocity (faster Figma execution)
- Explore visual directions outside your main project
- Produce shots for Dribbble (social presence, not case studies)

**Keep these separate from your portfolio case studies.** Daily UI produces *shots* (single polished screens). They show visual craft. Case studies show process. Your portfolio needs both, clearly labeled.

## The accessibility audit project

This is worth doing as a standalone case study and is rare enough in junior portfolios to be a real differentiator.

**The brief:** pick a real publicly accessible app or website. Run a systematic WCAG 2.2 AA audit. Propose redesigns for the failures.

**The deliverables:**
- A Stark accessibility audit report for the existing UI
- Annotated Figma screens showing each failure
- Proposed redesigns for each failure with rationale
- A written summary: severity, user impact, and remediation priority

This project shows you know WCAG 2.2, can use professional tools like Stark, think systematically, and care about inclusive design — all signals that stand out.

## Portfolio site

Your portfolio needs a home. Options in order of effort:

| Option | What you get | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| **Behance** | Free, indexed by Adobe, case-study friendly | **Free** |
| **Notion** | Good for long-form case studies; link from a short bio page | **Free** |
| **Framer** (hobby tier) | Real portfolio site with custom domain option; can host hi-fi prototypes | Free (watermarked) or ~$10/mo |
| **Custom built** | Full control; only worth it if you can actually build it cleanly | Free–$10/mo |

**Dribbble note:** Dribbble is for shots (single polished screens), not case studies. A good Dribbble presence builds social proof; it doesn't replace a case-study portfolio. The free tier limits uploads — don't pay for Pro until you have shots worth sharing.

- [ ] 3–4 case studies live on your portfolio site
- [ ] Each case study has: problem → research → decision → outcome
- [ ] At least one case study includes real research (not made-up users)
- [ ] At least one case study shows iteration (a design that changed)
- [ ] One accessibility-focused piece (audit or redesign)
